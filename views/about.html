Who was Fred Rogers?

Melissa Crowton, Google Doodle Team doodler and project director, still remembers the episode featuring author and illustrator Eric Carle that has inspired her throughout her career.


“I was really impacted in the way that Mister Rogers wanted his audience to feel,” she says in the Google Doodle behind-the-scenes video. “You have it within you to make something special.”


And that’s what she has done with the stop-motion video of Rogers and the Land of Make Believe.


It’s a colorful, soulful animation. From the moment you see Rogers hop off the trolley in his green cardigan, your heart sings, “It’s a beautiful day in this neighborhood, a beautiful day for a neighbor, would you be mine, could you be mine?”


The show didn’t first air until Feb. 19, 1968 — but it was filmed 51 years ago on Sept. 21, 1967. As Morgan Neville’s documentary “Won’t You Be My Neighbor” reminded us this summer: Rogers was radical.


That first week of the show subtly tackled heavy political issues. King Friday, the Land of Make-Believe’s mad monarch, built a fence around his castle — almost like a wall. People had to give their name and rank if they want to enter. Sound familiar?


Like most great thinkers, Rogers was ahead of his time and his ideals are still relevant. The show wasn’t as diverse as “Sesame Street,” but in many ways it was far more edgy. He subtly tackled war, gender roles, and integration.


Not long after the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr., when black people had acid thrown on them to keep them out of swimming pools, Rogers soaked his feet in a wading pool with Officer Clemmons, played by Francois Clemmons — one of the first black characters on children’s television. Rogers sent Lady Elaine Fairchilde into space. There was black woman mayor, Mayor Maggie of Southwood. He was very intentional in how he demonstrated inclusivity.


Today, kids tune into the Rogers spinoff, “Daniel Tiger’s Neighborhood.” But fans still stream the original show on Amazon Prime and iTunes. PBS Kids still has full episodes online.


Fred Rogers Productions called the Google Doodle “a gift to share his memory.”


“His legacy is really about understanding children, caring about them, but most importantly respecting them,” says Margy Whitmer, Fred Rogers Productions media producer. “It’s really about kindness.”


We could all use some of that. The animation ends with Rogers’ most consistent message of all — loving and being yourself.


“You help to make each day a special day by just you being yourself,” he says from a TV screen in the Google Doodle as children watch. “There’s nobody else in the whole world who’s exactly like you, and people can like you exactly as you are.”


Rogers died of stomach cancer in 2003, just two years after he finished his 31st and final season. But as this Google Doodle proves, he is forever our neighbor.
